Best information about this mountain

Mount Zuqualla (also spelled Zuquala, Zikwala, or Chuqqaala) is an extinct volcano located in the Oromia Region of Ethiopia. Situated in the Ada'a Chukala woreda of the (East) Shewa Zone, it rises from the plain approximately 30 kilometers (19 miles) south of Bishoftu¹. With a height of 2,989 meters (9,806 feet), it is known for its crater lake, Lake Dembel. The elliptical crater lake has a maximum diameter of about one kilometer, and the trail around the crater is approximately 6 kilometers long. Both the mountain and the lake hold religious significance for the Oromo people living nearby. An Oromo proverb reflects the ambivalent attitude toward the mountain's holiness: "Those who live far away worship it, those who live nearby plow it." The lake within the crater features an island called Tulluu Irreechaa, which is said to have been founded by Abba Gadaa of Tuulama on the site of a hermitage used by Saint Mercurius. In 1531, Imam Ahmad Gragn destroyed the monastery on the mountain and looted a church at its foot. Later, two churches were built at the monastery: one dedicated to St. Gebre Menfas by Menelik II in 1880, and the other dedicated to Kidane Mihret during the reign of Haile Selassie. Various other holy sites are found around Mount Zuqualla, mostly in the form of rock formations. The monastery itself hosts a biannual festival. In 1881, explorers Orazio Antinori, Antonelli, and Antonio Cecchi used Zuqualla to determine various geographical locations. Additionally, Dr. Scott, on behalf of Cambridge University and the British Museum, collected a valuable entomological collection near Zuqualla in 1926. Notably, during the attempted 1960 Ethiopian coup, three of the leaders fled to Zuqualla from the capital, where the Moja family owned land. Two of them lost their lives on December 24, while Mengistu Neway, seriously wounded, was captured and brought to the capital for trial².
