Lega Oda Archaeological Site, located around 38 km southwest of Dire Dawa in Ethiopia. Here are five paragraphs about this intriguing site:
Lega Oda Cave, nestled in the side of a limestone hill, has captured the interest of researchers for decades. Dating back to the Upper Pleistocene and Holocene periods, this cave offers a unique glimpse into the prehistoric past. Its rock paintings and evidence of a stone tool industry provide valuable insights into early human culture and communication.
Lega Oda is subdivided into two floors. The upper floor spans approximately 60 meters in length, three meters in height, and four meters in depth. In contrast, the lower floor is shorter, reaching only two meters in height. The cave houses around 600 rock paintings, each telling a story of ancient creativity and symbolism.
The paintings within Lega Oda Cave are a testament to humanity's earliest attempts at visual expression. Using red, brown, and black pigments, our ancestors left their mark on the cave walls. These ancient artists depicted domestic animals, hands, and other symbols, providing clues to their daily lives, beliefs, and communication methods.
Beyond art, Lega Oda yielded a rich assemblage of stone tools and faunal remains. Researchers have uncovered tens of thousands of lithic artifacts, including tools used by early humans. Among the discoveries is a lower jawbone from Homo sapiens, dating between 60,000 and 77,000 years ago. The cave also hosts agile mammals like hares and hyraxes.
Lega Oda stands as a prehistoric art studio, where our ancestors left their mark on the walls, bridging the gap between ancient cultures and our modern understanding.
